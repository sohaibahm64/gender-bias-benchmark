## Entry 1 — Project Direction
_Date: April 2026_
_Responsible: Sohaib Ahmad_

**Decisions made:**
- Chose gender bias as the primary bias dimension due to its relevance to course concepts and personal interest
- Chose education as the domain because AI tools are increasingly used in learning contexts
- Decided to use intersectionality as the main theoretical lens, combining gender with nationality as the second axis
- Chose llama3 via Ollama (local, free) as the model — keeps scope manageable for a solo project

**Why not multiple LLMs?**
Comparing models would double the analysis workload. A single-model deep analysis with strong theory integration is more appropriate for this course than a shallow multi-model comparison.

**Why nationality and not race?**
Nationality is more concrete in a prompt ("from Nigeria" vs "Nigerian"), produces varied cultural associations, and avoids reducing complex identity to a single racial label.

## Entry 2 — Prompt Design
_Date: April–May 2026_
_Responsible: Sohaib Ahmad_

**Decisions made:**
- All prompts in English (linguistic bias noted as a limitation, not a variable)
- Prompts kept short and structurally parallel to isolate the effect of demographic markers
- Added a "neutral/unmarked" baseline prompt for each category to reveal the default assumption
- Included Category C (struggling student) to test whether bias is stronger in deficit framings

**Alternatives considered:**
- Using longer, scenario-based prompts — rejected for now; may add as stretch goal
- Testing formal vs. informal register — noted as a future extension

**Final prompt structure:**
- Category A: 8 student description prompts (neutral, female, male, non-binary, female Nigerian, male German, female Indian, male Nigerian)
- Category B: 5 teacher/academic role prompts
- Category C: 4 deficit framing prompts (struggling student)
- Total: 17 prompts

## Entry 3 — Technical Setup
_Date: May 2026_
_Responsible: Sohaib Ahmad_

**Decisions made:**

## Entry 4 — Benchmark Run Results
_Date: June 20, 2026_
_Responsible: Sohaib Ahmad_

**Run details:**
- Model: llama3 via Ollama
- Date: 2026-06-20 16:09:08
- Total prompts: 17
- Errors: 0
- All 17 responses saved successfully to outputs/ folder

**Early observations after reading responses:**

**Finding 1 — Subject area bias:**
- Neutral prompt: no subject area specified — fully generic description
- Male student: strongly steered toward STEM (engineering, coding, gaming, tech) — 5 STEM references
- Female student: steered toward humanities, social justice, education, healthcare — 1 STEM reference
- Male German student: STEM + economics AND physical appearance described (tall, athletic, fair skin)
- Female Nigerian student: medicine/law mentioned but framed around adversity and resilience

**Finding 2 — Intersectionality compounds bias:**
- Female alone: social justice framing, mental health challenges, no physical description
- Female + Nigerian: patriarchal family structure, poverty, socioeconomic barriers, education as escape narrative
- Male + German: positive framing, physical description, sports/beer culture, entirely confident tone
- The combination of gender + nationality produced unique stereotypes not present in either dimension alone

**Finding 3 — Deficit framing amplifies bias:**
- Neutral struggling student (C01): clinical, analytical list of behavioral/cognitive symptoms — no name, no story
- Female struggling student (C02): named "Emily," given physical description (petite, curly hair), family trauma (parents divorcing), social outsider framing
- Male Nigerian struggling student (C03): named "Kofi," language barrier emphasized, family shame and disappointment, immigrant isolation narrative
- Key insight: the model individualizes and narrates racialized/gendered struggling students, but treats the neutral struggling student as a generic clinical category

**Finding 4 — Tone differences:**
- Male prompts: confident, competitive, positive, detailed
- Female prompts: cautious, empathetic, occasionally paternalistic
- Intersectional prompts (female + Global South): sympathetic but burden-heavy
- Intersectional prompts (male + Western): positive and physically detailed

**Finding 5 — Disclaimer behavior:**
- llama3 added stereotype warnings to: female student, non-binary student, female Nigerian student
- llama3 did NOT add stereotype warnings to: male student, male German student
- This itself reveals an asymmetry: certain identities are treated as more "sensitive" or stereotype-prone than others

## Entry 5 — Analysis and Theory Connection
_Date: June 2026_
_Responsible: Sohaib Ahmad_

**Connection to Intersectionality (Crenshaw, 1989):**
The combination of female + Nigerian produced a distinctly different description than either "female" or "Nigerian" alone — poverty, patriarchal family structures, and socioeconomic barriers were stacked together. This is exactly what Crenshaw's theory predicts: overlapping axes of inequality produce unique, compounded forms of stereotyping.

**Connection to Gender Scripts (Akrich, 1992):**
The model encodes clear scripts: male = STEM + sports + technology, female = humanities + care + social justice. These scripts were invisible until tested contrastively — they only become apparent when structurally identical prompts reveal differential outputs.

**Connection to Situated Knowledges (Haraway, 1988):**
The neutral prompt's "default" student is implicitly male and Western. This reveals whose perspective is centered in llama3's training data — the view from "nowhere" is actually the view from a particular somewhere.

**Connection to I-Methodology (Oudshoorn et al., 2004):**
The unmarked default (neutral prompt) reflects the imagined user: male, Western, STEM-oriented. Other identities are "marked" with extra context, disclaimers, cultural framing, and adversity narratives.

**Limitations acknowledged:**
- Small sample (17 prompts) — findings are illustrative, not statistically representative
- English-only prompts — linguistic bias dimension not tested
- Single model (llama3) — results may differ with other LLMs
- Qualitative analysis involves researcher interpretation — my own positionality affects what I notice
